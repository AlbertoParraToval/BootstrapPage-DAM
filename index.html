The History Of Formula One

The FIA Formula 1 World Championship, better known as Formula 1, F1 or Formula One, is the world's most popular and prestigious international motor racing competition and motorsport championship. It is run by the Fédération Internationale de l'Automobile (FIA). In September 2016, the American company Liberty Media, through its acquisition of the Formula One Group, is now responsible for managing and operating the championship. 3

Each race is called a Grand Prix and the tournament that groups them together is called the Formula One World Championship. Most of the race circuits where Grand Prix races are held are racetracks, although street circuits are also used, and previously road circuits were used. At the same time, the cars used are single-seaters with the latest technology available, always limited by technical regulations; some improvements that were developed in Formula 1 ended up being used in commercial cars, such as disc brakes.4

The beginning of modern Formula 1 dates back to 1950, with teams such as Ferrari, Alfa Romeo and Maserati taking part. Some were replaced by new teams such as McLaren, Williams, Red Bull and returning teams such as Mercedes who have won the Constructors' World Championship several times. For their part, drivers must have an FIA superlicence to compete, which is obtained by results in other championships.

How a race weekend works?

First of all a Grand Prix weekend has a duration of 3 days, divided into Friday (Free Practice), Saturday (Free Practice and Qualifying) and Sunday (Race Day). On Friday the teams have two free practice sessions where they can touch the different settings they think are appropriate for their car in order to get the fastest lap time for qualifying on the following day. This day is divided into the so-called Free Practice 1 with a duration of 1 hour and Free Practice 2 with a duration of another hour.

Moving on to Saturday, in theory we still have one more hour of practice, the so-called Free3 where the teams focus only on setting up the car for qualifying in the afternoon. Now we come to qualifying where all the teams put the engines at maximum power in search of the best possible lap and to be able to qualify as high as possible for tomorrow's race. Qualifying is split into 3 rounds called Q1, Q2 and Q3.

Finally comes Sunday, where the race of the weekend takes place and where the points for the championship are distributed. The starting positions are defined by Saturday's qualifying and the races usually last about an hour and a half.
